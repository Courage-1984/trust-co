01 Working with Trust Co

Does using this website or reading the FAQ create a lawyer–client relationship?
No. Browsing our site, downloading general materials, or reading these answers does not by itself establish an attorney–client (or advocate–client) relationship. A formal engagement is agreed in writing with clear scope and terms. Until then, do not assume that anything here — or in an initial email — is privileged in the same way as advice under a confirmed mandate.
How do I instruct Trust Co?
The usual route is to contact us via the contact form, the navbar callback request, or by phone or email. We will clarify conflicts, outline how we can help, and send engagement terms (including fee arrangements) for your acceptance. Work that requires court appearances or formal steps only proceeds once the relationship and scope are clear.
Is an initial enquiry confidential?
We treat enquiries seriously and handle personal information in line with our Privacy Policy and POPIA. Legal professional privilege attaches to confidential communications for the purpose of obtaining legal advice once a proper practitioner–client relationship exists and the communication qualifies under the rules — not every informal contact will meet that test. Avoid sending identity numbers or highly sensitive documents through unsecured web forms; we will agree a secure channel when appropriate.
Do you act for individuals as well as companies?
Yes. We advise directors, shareholders, creditors, trustees, beneficiaries, and individuals facing insolvency-related or fiduciary issues. Who we can act for in a specific matter depends on conflict checks — for example, we cannot simultaneously represent opposing parties in the same dispute.
We are based outside Gauteng — can you still assist?
Trust Co is based in Gauteng, but much of our work has a national dimension — Companies Act processes, business rescue, and trust matters often involve stakeholders across South Africa. We use video calls and structured document workflows where appropriate. Travel or local co-counsel may be needed for certain court appearances or filings.
How quickly will you respond to an enquiry?
We aim to acknowledge routine website and email enquiries within one business day. Mark your matter urgent if creditor action, liquidity failure, or a court date is imminent — we triage those as a priority, subject to capacity and conflicts. Nothing on this site guarantees a same-day response.
Will you tell me your fees up front?
Yes, in principle. After we understand the scope, we explain fee options (for example hourly, fixed phases, or capped estimates where appropriate) and disbursements. Complex or fast-moving matters may require staged budgets. You should not commit to a mandate until you are comfortable with the proposed terms.

02 Liquidation & winding-up

What is liquidation?
Liquidation is the process of winding up a company’s affairs: realising assets, paying creditors in the order prescribed by law, and ultimately dissolving the entity. A licensed liquidator is appointed to administer the estate. The precise steps depend on whether the liquidation is voluntary or court-driven and whether the company is insolvent or solvent.
What is the difference between voluntary and compulsory liquidation?
Voluntary liquidation is initiated by the company (typically by board and/or shareholder resolution) when shareholders and directors decide to wind up — which may be for insolvency or, if solvent, for restructuring or exit. Compulsory liquidation follows a court order, commonly on a creditor’s application when a company cannot pay its debts and no other remedy is in place.
Can a solvent company be liquidated?
Yes. Members’ voluntary liquidation is used when the company is solvent: creditors are paid in full, and any surplus goes to shareholders. It is a common tool for orderly closure, group simplification, or after a sale of business. It is not a substitute for business rescue if the goal is to keep trading.
What does a liquidator do?
The liquidator takes control of the company’s assets and affairs, investigates the company’s financial position, collects claims, sells assets, adjudicates creditor claims, pays distributions according to statutory ranking, and pursues recoveries (such as voidable dispositions) where the facts and law support it. Directors’ powers are largely displaced during liquidation.
Can directors be held personally liable after liquidation?
Potentially, yes — depending on conduct. South African company and insolvency law includes provisions on reckless trading, misuse of company property, and certain personal liability for debts (for example where directors signed personal sureties). Liquidators may also investigate antecedent transactions. Early legal advice helps directors understand exposure and document decisions defensibly.
Where do secured creditors stand?
Secured creditors generally enforce or realise their security in accordance with the security agreement and applicable law, subject to the liquidator’s oversight of the estate as a whole. The interplay between security, preferential claims, and concurrent creditors is fact-specific — security documents and registration must be reviewed.
How long does liquidation take?
Timelines vary widely. A simple solvent wind-down may conclude in months; contentious insolvent estates with litigation, complex asset sales, or disputed claims can run for years. The liquidator’s reporting obligations and Master’s office processes also affect duration.

03 Business rescue

What is business rescue?
Business rescue is a Chapter 6 process under the Companies Act 71 of 2008. A financially distressed company is placed under supervision and a Business Rescue Practitioner (BRP) is appointed to develop and implement a plan to reorganise the business and restore viability — or, if that fails, to achieve a better return than immediate liquidation would.
When should directors consider business rescue?
Directors must monitor solvency and liquidity. If the company is unlikely to pay its debts as they fall due in the next six months, or is likely to become insolvent in that period, the board should obtain advice promptly. Delay can narrow options, increase personal risk, and damage stakeholder confidence. See also our note on business rescue services.
What is the “moratorium” in business rescue?
On commencement of business rescue, a temporary stay generally applies on legal proceedings against the company (subject to exceptions in the Act). The purpose is to give the BRP breathing room to stabilise operations and negotiate a rescue plan without piecemeal enforcement dismantling the estate.
Who approves a business rescue plan?
The BRP prepares a plan that meets statutory requirements. Creditors vote in prescribed classes; shareholders also have a role in defined circumstances. If voting thresholds are not met, the BRP may seek court intervention. The details are technical and time-sensitive.
What if business rescue fails?
The BRP may apply to end rescue and place the company in liquidation, or creditors or other affected persons may seek court orders. The company may also exit rescue successfully if the plan is implemented. We help stakeholders understand options at each stage.
Is business rescue always cheaper than liquidation?
Not necessarily. Rescue involves practitioner fees, possible trading losses, and creditor compromises. The economic question is whether rescue preserves more value than a break-up sale — that requires modelling and honest assessment. We do not promise outcomes; we help you structure the process professionally.

04 Trusts & estates

What is a trust under South African law?
A trust is a legal relationship (not a separate “person” in every context) created when a founder places assets under the control of trustees for the benefit of beneficiaries, on terms set out in a trust deed. The Trust Property Control Act and Master’s office supervision frame administration and registration requirements.
Discretionary trust vs vested trust — what is the difference?
In broad terms, a discretionary trust gives trustees power to decide whether, when, and how much to distribute among a class of beneficiaries. A vested interest means a beneficiary has a defined right to income or capital (subject to the deed). Tax, estate duty, and creditor considerations differ — structure must match your objectives.
Can trustees be removed or replaced?
The trust deed is the starting point: it may allow founder or beneficiary powers to appoint and remove trustees, or require court or Master involvement. Disputes about breach of fiduciary duty may also lead to removal. We advise trustees and beneficiaries on lawful processes.
What is a “sham” trust?
Courts may disregard trust form where the arrangement is a facade — for example, where the founder effectively retained sole control and benefit, or the trust was used to defeat creditors unlawfully. Allegations of sham trusts are fact-intensive; proper governance and documentation matter.
How do trusts interact with estate duty and donations tax?
Transfers into trusts, loan accounts, and deemed property rules can trigger donations tax, estate duty, or anti-avoidance provisions depending on timing, valuation, and structure. We coordinate with tax practitioners where specialist input is required — Trust Co focuses on the legal architecture and fiduciary compliance.
Do you draft trust deeds and wills?
We assist with trust formation, amendments, and related agreements, and we advise on estate alignment. Will execution formalities in South Africa are strict; we can guide you on structure and refer or collaborate on execution where appropriate. Tell us your objectives and family/commercial context.

05 Directors, creditors & advisory

What is “reckless trading” and why does it matter?
Directors must not allow the business to trade in a manner that is reckless, grossly negligent, or intended to defraud creditors. Breach can lead to personal liability and other sanctions. If the company is in financial distress, boards should document decisions, seek professional advice, and consider business rescue or orderly wind-down where appropriate.
What is a section 129 notice (in broad terms)?
Under the Companies Act, a board that concludes the company is financially distressed must notify affected parties and decide on a course — which may include filing for business rescue or resolving to liquidate if rescue is not viable. The wording, timing, and filings are statutory; get advice before you sign resolutions.
I am a creditor — should I vote for a business rescue plan?
That depends on the plan’s treatment of your claim, security, and the alternative (often a low recovery in liquidation). We help creditors analyse plans, attend meetings, and preserve objections or appeals where the law allows.
Can you advise both sides in the same dispute?
Generally, no. We run conflict checks before accepting instructions. If a new matter conflicts with an existing client’s interests, we will decline or limit scope in line with professional rules. Do not send confidential details until we confirm we can act.
Do you assist with compromises or schemes of arrangement?
Yes, where appropriate alongside or instead of formal business rescue — for example creditor compromises, related company restructuring, or negotiated workouts. The right tool depends on urgency, stakeholder numbers, and asset complexity.
